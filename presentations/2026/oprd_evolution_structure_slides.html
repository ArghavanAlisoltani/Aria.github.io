Why OprD is central to resistance biology

OprD is a substrate-specific outer membrane porin of P. aeruginosa. Its native role is uptake of basic amino acids and related compounds, but that same route is exploited by imipenem and some other carbapenems.

Core evolutionary idea: mutations in oprD are not just “resistance mutations.” They alter a membrane protein whose function, folding, pore chemistry, and expression are all under selection.

Architecture
Slide 3 / 10

Structural architecture of OprD

What the structure looks like

  • Monomeric 18-stranded β-barrel spanning the outer membrane.
  • A narrow eyelet / constriction zone determines substrate selectivity.
  • Extracellular loops shape access, recognition, and pore chemistry.
  • The internal channel is asymmetric: chemistry matters as much as pore size.
  • Natural substrates and carbapenems overlap only partially in how they engage the pore.
Structure determines which mutations are likely to be tolerated, disruptive, or substrate-specific
Mechanism
Slide 4 / 10

How OprD’s structure controls function

1. Pore geometry

The channel is not a simple open tube. Loop-defined narrowing creates a physically and chemically selective passage.

2. Charge distribution

Residues in the eyelet and nearby loops create electrostatic preferences that favor some molecules and exclude others.

3. Dynamic access

Surface loops likely modulate recognition, dwell time, and steric entry, especially for carbapenems vs natural ligands.

Why loops 2 and 3 keep appearing

  • Experimental work repeatedly implicates L2 and L3 in imipenem binding / passage.
  • Mutations here can be subtle: not every missense change destroys folding, but many can alter permeability.
  • This is why structure-aware interpretation is more informative than labeling all missense variants equally.

Why the C-terminus matters

  • Late truncations can remove terminal β-strands.
  • That can compromise barrel closure, membrane insertion, or overall stability.
  • A mutation may therefore increase resistance by causing porin loss through misfolding, not by changing only local binding chemistry.
Function emerges from pore size + chemistry + fold stability + expression
Evolutionary paths

Major evolutionary routes affecting OprD

Mutation class Likely structural consequence Expected phenotype logic
Frameshift / premature stop / large indel Truncation, failed barrel assembly, unstable protein, or complete loss Often strongest route to OprD deficiency and clear imipenem effect
Promoter / upstream changes Protein sequence may remain intact, but abundance falls Resistance through reduced expression rather than altered pore geometry
Missense in eyelet / loops Changes in charge, sterics, substrate recognition, or gating behavior Can shift permeability without complete porin loss
Missense in barrel core Destabilized fold, altered strand packing, membrane insertion problems Possible hidden loss-of-function despite conservative-looking sequence change
Lineage-specific intact variants Different but folded OprD proteins, sometimes preserving native function Important caution: diversity alone does not imply resistance

Key lesson: OprD evolution includes both binary loss-of-function events and graded structural tuning. The second category is where protein structure adds the most value.

Sequence diversity must be partitioned into expression, folding, and pore-function mechanisms
Selection and constraint
Slide 6 / 10

How to think about OprD evolution mechanistically

Competing selective pressures

  • Nutrient uptake favors a functional, selective pore.
  • Antibiotic exposure favors reduced carbapenem entry.
  • Membrane biogenesis / stability constrains which sequence changes remain viable.
  • Clone background matters because efflux, β-lactamases, and cell-wall changes can modify the apparent effect of OprD mutations.

Expected evolutionary signature

  • Global purifying constraint on residues needed for fold and native transport.
  • Repeated disruptive mutations in drug-exposed settings because complete loss can be adaptive.
  • Localized adaptive missense changes in loops / eyelet if they reduce drug passage while preserving enough physiological function.
  • Homoplasy may occur if multiple lineages independently converge on similar structural solutions.

Why structure changes interpretation
Slide 7 / 10

Why structure-aware analysis is necessary

Sequence-only pitfall

A missense mutation can look minor in alignment space but still strongly alter pore electrostatics, loop shape, or barrel stability.

Classifier pitfall

Tools that primarily flag truncation, absence, indels, and promoter disruption are excellent for obvious OprD loss, but they do not fully resolve the gray zone of missense variants.

Structural opportunity

Mapping mutations onto the 3D fold lets you ask whether a residue sits in the eyelet, surface loop, β-strand core, or terminal assembly region.

Best interpretation logic

  1. Classify mutation type first: null, regulatory, missense, indel.
  2. Map residue(s) onto structure and topology.
  3. Estimate effect on fold stability and pore chemistry.
  4. Interpret in the context of carbapenem phenotype and background resistance mechanisms.

What this helps you separate

  • True intact but functionally altered alleles
  • Neutral lineage polymorphisms
  • Folding-disruptive substitutions
  • Pore-tuning substitutions likely to alter imipenem entry
Structure is most informative in the missense-rich middle ground
Example mutation logic
Slide 8 / 10

Three common structural outcomes of OprD evolution

A. Porin loss

Frameshift / stop-gain / major indel

Expected outcome: incomplete barrel, failed membrane insertion, absent or unstable OprD.

Often gives the clearest imipenem-associated resistance jump.

B. Selectivity shift

Missense in loops 2/3 or the eyelet

Expected outcome: altered steric or electrostatic environment with preserved overall fold.

May differentially affect native substrates and carbapenems.

C. Hidden functional loss

Missense in β-strand packing or terminal strands

Expected outcome: subtle destabilization that looks “intact” by sequence but behaves like a defective porin.

Important reason to combine sequence, structure, and phenotype.

Different mutations can produce similar resistance phenotypes through different structural routes
Research workflow
Slide 9 / 10

How to study OprD evolution in a genome collection

Recommended analysis stack

  1. Extract oprD nucleotide + protein alleles from each isolate.
  2. Classify variants into intact, truncation, indel, promoter, and missense groups.
  3. Build a core-genome phylogeny and map OprD alleles onto the tree.
  4. Test for recurrent changes / homoplasy and lineage enrichment.
  5. Map amino-acid changes onto OprD structure (e.g., 3SY7-based coordinates).
  6. Stratify residues by topology: loop, eyelet, β-strand core, periplasmic turn, terminal strand.
  7. Combine with phenotype to separate likely causal vs background polymorphism.

High-value figures

  • Lollipop-on-structure plot of recurrent residues
  • Phylogeny + allele heatmap for convergence
  • Topology-resolved mutation density across OprD
  • Null vs missense phenotype comparison
  • 3D clustering of resistance-associated residues
  • dN/dS or site-level constraint split by structural class

Best conceptual output: a model showing that OprD evolution is shaped by where mutations occur in the structure, not just how many mutations are present.

Genome-scale work becomes much stronger when residue location is treated as a biological variable
Take-home points + references
Slide 10 / 10

Take-home message

OprD evolution is best understood as adaptive remodeling of a selective β-barrel channel under simultaneous pressure from nutrient acquisition, protein-fold constraints, and carbapenem exposure. The strongest studies therefore combine phylogeny, variant class, 3D structure, and phenotype.

Three ideas to remember

  • Null alleles are only part of the story.
  • Missense variants require structural interpretation.
  • Convergent evolution may occur at the level of protein topology, not just exact residue identity.
