0) Course overview

start here

This course teaches bacterial protein families from a structural-bioinformatics perspective. Instead of memorizing isolated genes, you learn a reusable framework: family → domains → fold → localization → biochemical mechanism → biological role → computational analysis.

What you will learn

The major protein families that appear repeatedly across bacterial genomes and how their structures explain their functions.

What you will code

How to retrieve annotations from UniProt, search structures in PDB, inspect domains, parse PDB files, scan motifs, and summarize family abundance.

What you will build

A small but realistic workflow for taking a genome or proteome and turning it into a family-level structural and functional summary.

The most important conceptual shift is this: the same biological function can be shared by many different proteins, but the same protein family usually carries a conserved core architecture, conserved motifs, and conserved mechanism.

1) Protein family, domain, fold, motif, and complex

core concepts
TermMeaningExample
Protein familyEvolutionarily related proteins with shared sequence and function patternsClass A beta-lactamases, LysR regulators, major facilitator transporters
Protein domainA modular structural/functional unit inside a proteinRossmann domain, HTH DNA-binding domain, peptidase M4 domain
FoldOverall 3D arrangement of secondary structuresTIM barrel, beta-propeller, alpha-helical bundle, outer-membrane beta-barrel
MotifShort conserved sequence or structural featureHXH metal-binding motif, Walker A P-loop, SXXK in serine enzymes
ComplexMulti-subunit assemblyRibosome, ATP synthase, BAM complex, RND efflux pump tripartite assembly

Why this matters

A family tells you evolutionary context; a domain tells you modular function; a fold tells you structural constraints; a motif often points to mechanism.

Common beginner mistake

Treating a gene name, a protein family, and a domain as if they were interchangeable. They are related but not identical.

3) Major structural archetypes in bacteria

structure

Alpha-helical membrane proteins

Found mainly in the cytoplasmic membrane. Typical for transporters, sensors, respiratory proteins, and signal transduction.

Outer-membrane beta-barrels

Common in Gram-negative bacteria. Used for porins, receptors, secretion-related proteins, and assembly factors.

Globular soluble enzymes

Typical in cytosol or periplasm. Often adopt Rossmann, TIM-barrel, alpha/beta hydrolase, or beta-sheet rich catalytic folds.

Filament proteins

Repeated subunits assemble into flagella, pili, secretion needles, and extracellular fibers.

Repeat proteins and scaffolds

Beta-propellers, HEAT/TPR-like repeats, or coiled coils often act as adaptors or assembly platforms.

Large multiprotein machines

Ribosome, ATP synthase, secretion systems, divisome, and cell-envelope biogenesis complexes.

4) Useful formulas and quantitative ideas

math

Structural and family analyses are built on a few simple quantitative ideas.

\[\text{Sequence identity} = \frac{\text{number of identical aligned residues}}{\text{alignment length}} \times 100\]

Sequence identity helps judge whether two proteins likely share a recent common ancestor, but family detection often works below obvious pairwise identity because profile models are more sensitive.

\[S = \sum_i \log \frac{P(x_i \mid \text{family model})}{P(x_i \mid \text{background})}\]

This is the logic behind profile-HMM scoring: residues that fit the family model raise the score.

\[v = \frac{V_{\max}[S]}{K_M + [S]}\]

When you compare enzyme families experimentally, Michaelis-Menten kinetics often links structure to catalytic performance.

\[\text{RMSD} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{N}\sum_{i=1}^{N}\left\|\mathbf r_i - \mathbf r_i' \right\|^2}\]

RMSD is one of the classic structure-comparison measures, though it should be interpreted with care for multi-domain proteins or flexible proteins.

6) Enzyme superfamilies and catalytic logic

enzymes

Many bacterial proteins are enzymes, but different enzyme families solve chemistry with recurring structural logic.

Rossmann-fold enzymes

Often bind NAD, NADP, or FAD. The fold is common in dehydrogenases and metabolic enzymes.

TIM-barrel enzymes

One of the most common enzyme scaffolds. Catalytic residues often cluster near the barrel mouth.

Alpha/beta hydrolases

Classic scaffold for lipases, esterases, and some serine enzymes. Often use a catalytic Ser-Asp/Glu-His triad.

Metalloenzymes

Use Zn, Fe, Mn, Mg, or other metals for hydrolysis, redox chemistry, and substrate positioning.

A protein family can be recognized from sequence, but the mechanism often becomes much clearer after you identify the catalytic fold and conserved residues.

10) Secretion systems and virulence proteins

virulence

Secretion systems are not single proteins but multi-protein families that assemble into machines.

Type I

Direct one-step export across both membranes using an ABC transporter, membrane fusion protein, and outer-membrane channel.

Type II

Exports folded periplasmic proteins through a secretin-based system and pseudopilus machinery.

Type III

Injectisome-like nanomachine delivering effectors into host cells.

Type VI

Contractile phage-tail-like machine that delivers toxic effectors into neighboring cells.

14) Translation, replication, and housekeeping families

housekeeping

Not every important protein family is a virulence or AMR factor. Essential housekeeping families provide the baseline physiology that everything else depends on.

  • Ribosomal proteins: small RNA-binding proteins that stabilize the ribosome and tune translation.
  • Translation factors: EF-Tu, EF-G, IFs, RFs, all with recurring nucleotide-binding and factor-associated folds.
  • Replication proteins: DnaA, DnaB, Pol III subunits, sliding clamps, ligases, primases.
  • Repair proteins: RecA, MutS, Uvr proteins, nucleases, helicases.
  • Division proteins: FtsZ tubulin-like GTPase, divisome-associated membrane factors, peptidoglycan remodeling proteins.
If you want a clean benchmark set for protein family classification, housekeeping families are often easier to validate because they are widespread and well curated.

16) How to study a bacterial protein family

workflow
  1. Start with a sequence or a set of sequences.
  2. Find curated names and evidence in UniProt.
  3. Find domains and family assignments with InterPro or profile-HMM tools.
  4. Look for experimentally determined or predicted structures in PDB/AlphaFold-style resources.
  5. Check localization: signal peptide, transmembrane helices, lipobox, or outer-membrane beta-barrel patterns.
  6. Identify catalytic or binding motifs.
  7. Compare presence/absence or variation across strains or species.
  8. Link sequence variation to structure and phenotype.
A strong family-level analysis is never “sequence only” or “structure only.” It combines sequence, domain architecture, localization, and phenotype.

17) Code: query UniProt for bacterial proteins

annotated code

Use curated UniProt metadata to retrieve protein names, genes, evidence, and organism information. The example below fetches reviewed entries for a bacterial gene family.

Annotated Python: search UniProt and save a TSV 
import requests
import pandas as pd

# Search query:
# - reviewed:true keeps Swiss-Prot entries only
# - gene:ampC looks for entries annotated with ampC
# - organism_name:"Pseudomonas aeruginosa" narrows to one species
query = 'reviewed:true AND gene:ampC AND organism_name:"Pseudomonas aeruginosa"'

# The UniProt REST API can return tabular fields directly.
url = "https://rest.uniprot.org/uniprotkb/search"
params = {
    "query": query,
    "format": "tsv",
    "fields": ",".join([
        "accession",
        "id",
        "protein_name",
        "gene_names",
        "organism_name",
        "length",
        "cc_function",
        "ft_domain"
    ]),
    "size": 100
}

r = requests.get(url, params=params, timeout=60)
r.raise_for_status()

with open("uniprot_ampc_pao1.tsv", "w") as f:
    f.write(r.text)

df = pd.read_csv("uniprot_ampc_pao1.tsv", sep="\t")
print(df.head())
print("Rows:", len(df))
Annotated curl example 
curl -G "https://rest.uniprot.org/uniprotkb/search" \

18) Code: domain annotation with InterPro and local HMMER

domains

Family analysis becomes much stronger when you move beyond pairwise BLAST and look at domains or profile matches.

Annotated shell: local HMMER scan against a protein-family database 
# Example: hmmscan against a local HMM library of protein families/domains
# Replace family_db.hmm with your downloaded HMM database.

hmmscan \
  --cpu 8 \
  --domtblout sample.domtblout \
  family_db.hmm \
  proteins.faa > sample.hmmscan.log

# The important file is sample.domtblout:
# one line per domain hit with scores, e-values, and alignment coordinates.
Annotated Python: parse hmmscan domtblout into a clean table 
import pandas as pd

rows = []
with open("sample.domtblout") as f:
    for line in f:
        if line.startswith("#") or not line.strip():
            continue
        parts = line.split()
        rows.append({
            "target_name": parts[0],     # family/domain model
            "query_name": parts[3],      # protein sequence
            "full_evalue": parts[6],
            "full_score": parts[7],
            "dom_evalue": parts[12],
            "ali_start": parts[17],
            "ali_end": parts[18],
            "env_start": parts[19],
            "env_end": parts[20]
        })

df = pd.DataFrame(rows)
df["full_evalue"] = df["full_evalue"].astype(float)
df["full_score"] = df["full_score"].astype(float)

best = df.sort_values(["query_name", "full_evalue", "full_score"]).groupby("query_name").head(1)
print(best.head())

best.to_csv("best_family_calls.tsv", sep="\t", index=False)
One short motif hit is not always enough to call a full family. Look at whole-domain coverage, architecture, and biological plausibility.

19) Code: search and inspect structures from PDB

structures

Structure helps answer whether a family forms a channel, enzyme pocket, oligomer, or filament subunit.

Annotated Python: search RCSB PDB for a protein name or gene 
import requests
import json

query = {
    "query": {
        "type": "terminal",
        "service": "text",
        "parameters": {
            "attribute": "rcsb_entity_source_organism.rcsb_scientific_name",
            "operator": "exact_match",
            "value": "Pseudomonas aeruginosa"
        }
    },
    "return_type": "entry",
    "request_options": {"pager": {"start": 0, "rows": 10}}
}

r.raise_for_status()
data = r.json()

ids = [x["identifier"] for x in data.get("result_set", [])]

Annotated Python: fetch metadata for a specific PDB entry 
import requests

pdb_id = "1XYZ"  # replace with a real PDB ID you found
url = f"https://data.rcsb.org/rest/v1/core/entry/{pdb_id}"

r = requests.get(url, timeout=60)
r.raise_for_status()

20) Code: analyze residues around an active site or ligand

structure parsing

One practical way to understand a protein family is to identify residues around a ligand, metal, or catalytic residue.

Annotated Python: list residues near a ligand using Biopython 
from Bio.PDB import PDBParser, NeighborSearch, Selection

pdb_file = "protein_complex.pdb"
ligand_resname = "LIG"
cutoff = 5.0

parser = PDBParser(QUIET=True)
structure = parser.get_structure("x", pdb_file)
model = structure[0]

all_atoms = Selection.unfold_entities(model, "A")
ns = NeighborSearch(all_atoms)

ligand_atoms = []
for chain in model:
    for residue in chain:
        if residue.get_resname().strip() == ligand_resname:
            ligand_atoms.extend(list(residue.get_atoms()))

if not ligand_atoms:
    raise ValueError("Ligand not found")

nearby = set()
for atom in ligand_atoms:
    for hit in ns.search(atom.coord, cutoff, level="A"):
        nearby.add(hit.get_parent())

for residue in sorted(nearby, key=lambda r: (r.get_parent().id, r.id[1])):
    print(residue.get_parent().id, residue.get_resname(), residue.id[1])

You can adapt this for metal ions, catalytic serines, or pore-lining residues in channels and porins.

21) Code: motif scan across FASTA files

motifs

Many bacterial families have informative motifs. A simple motif scan is not a full classifier, but it can help triage candidates.

Annotated Python: scan for simple family-related motifs 
import re
from Bio import SeqIO

# Example motifs:
# Walker A / P-loop NTPase-like motif: [AGX4GKST]-style simplified as [AGX]XXXXGKS is too broad.
# We use a common simplified regex below for teaching purposes.
motifs = {
    "P_loop_like": re.compile(r"[AGX][A-Z]{4}GK[ST]"),
    "HXH_metal_binding": re.compile(r"H.AH"),   # teaching example only
    "SXXK_serine_enzyme": re.compile(r"S..K")
}

for record in SeqIO.parse("proteins.faa", "fasta"):
    seq = str(record.seq)
    hits = []
    for motif_name, pattern in motifs.items():
        for m in pattern.finditer(seq):
            hits.append((motif_name, m.start()+1, m.group()))
    if hits:
        print(record.id, hits)
Motifs alone can overcall families. Always combine motif hits with domain architecture, length, localization, and alignment evidence.

22) Code: summarize family counts across genomes or isolates

comparative genomics

Once you have family calls for each protein, you can summarize counts across isolates. This is useful for pangenome-style questions and phenotype-linked family enrichment.

Annotated Python: family count matrix from protein calls 
import pandas as pd

# Example input columns:
# isolate, protein_id, family
df = pd.read_csv("protein_family_calls.tsv", sep="\t")

count_table = (
    df.groupby(["isolate", "family"])
      .size()
      .unstack(fill_value=0)
      .reset_index()

print(count_table.head())

# Optional: find families enriched in resistant isolates

24) Common interpretation mistakes

troubleshooting
MistakeWhy it is riskyBetter approach
Calling a family from one short motifMany motifs are convergent or low-specificityUse domains, full-length alignment, and architecture
Assuming every homolog has the same functionFamily members can specializeInspect active-site residues, localization, and context
Ignoring localizationMembrane/surface proteins behave differently from cytosolic enzymesAlways annotate compartment first
Assuming one structure explains all family membersLoops, oligomers, and insertions varyUse representative structures across subfamilies
Using sequence identity aloneRemote homologs can share fold with low identityUse profile methods and structure comparison when needed

  2. Start with a short list of families you care about: AMR, virulence, transport, regulation, or metabolism.
  3. Retrieve curated entries from UniProt.
  4. Add domains and structures.
  5. Build a per-isolate family matrix.
  6. Interpret the families using structure and localization, not just names.
The best beginner-to-intermediate project is usually not “all bacterial proteins.” It is a focused comparison of 5–20 families tied to one question.
